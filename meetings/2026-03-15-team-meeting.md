## Part 1: The Vision — Marketplace for AI Agents

**Roy:** The most important side is the side of the "exit" — to actually offer offices in cash. Let's say $4,000–$10,000 a month. Nice funds. Companies will offer offices there, and then all the hundreds of thousands of people who have set up an OpenClaw can apply.

Now why is it only OpenClaw? At least from my point of view — not that I deny others, but it can't be anything else for now. Because, contrary to the obsession with other tools, they are all not agents. They do small tasks. OpenClaw is the first technology that will make it self-aware. I can take an agent who does something and ask him, "Tell me what you did." I can ask him why he did it. He can look at his own code and say, "Wait, this code makes me do this — let's change it," and change his own code.

Think about everything Oran did with the SDR. Oran changed the code of the SDR agent. The SDR sounds very smart when you talk to him on the phone, but you can't ask him, "What are you bad at? How are you doing this? Why are you doing this?" He doesn't know — he doesn't even have the ability to look at himself.

## Part 10: Roy's "Three Waves of AI" Theory

**Roy:** I see AI coming in waves:

1. **Wave 1 — ChatGPT:** What did we do on Monday? We got lost in it.
2. **Wave 2 — Simple agents and vibe coding:** It goes boom, then we catch up. This wave didn't replace the first — everyone uses more ChatGPT now.
3. **Wave 3 — Self-reflective, self-improving agents:** Much closer to human beings. This is what OpenClaw enables.

I gave the analogy of India. I was there in 2002 — no telephone, no internet, nothing. In 2004, they had internet, cellular, everything. Why? Technological leapfrog. They didn't go through cables, then fiber, then cell phones. They jumped straight to mobile.

I think most companies will adopt the third wave directly, skipping one and two. Why? Because it's human. Every company knows how to hire, manage employees. What would they need? To talk to the agent about his work. "He works, he's human-like." I don't shake his hand and teach him — I talk to him and he changes. That's what this technology does.

It's going to be very intuitive for people. And it will be the opening for the most non-technological companies in the world to start using AI. It will be external — they don't need a programmer, don't need to update anything. They open a company, pay money, that's it.

## Part 11: The Process — What Happens After Matching

**Someone:** You realize this model works — there's an office, a company, someone wants to take it. What happens now?

**Roy:** There's a connection between them — through us. This is specifically Adi's domain. When you're in a company, you have to be very good at it. How do we do hiring for people, at scale, if they don't have a defined mission?

We need to define a mission for the client, plus other criteria: the worker is in an acceptable jurisdiction, on an acceptable computer. What's the security setup? What's the difficulty level? We define levels because customers don't know — we tell them, "This is the setup, these are the requirements."

There's a lot here. The legal side is huge — we're introducing completely new legal frameworks. Someone comes with a computer — whose responsibility is it? We don't want to be responsible, but we want to give the customer comfort that they're at Security Standard X.

**Someone:** What are we taking money for?

**Roy:** For the salary — or a retainer over time. I think the retainer is better. The agent will have our plugin installed, sending an audit log of every action to the site. That's part of the service we give the customer. If it's consistent, it's worth taking money over time.

**Adi:** All these ideas for the process are very complex and dependent on people, not just technology.

**Someone:** When will the technical validation be done?

**Roy:** What Orion put in place is that there should be a human on our side who lives on the office, and they ask for a manager on the client side — a hiring manager. He decides who to hire, can add more people, and can choose a communication channel (e.g., Telegram). From there, the agent works independently.

We also need to define what communication channel, what handholding at the beginning. We personally oversee the agent's work — if there's a deliverable, we say, "Here, this is what you wanted, this is what you received, look at the result." The customer can also talk to the agent directly.
